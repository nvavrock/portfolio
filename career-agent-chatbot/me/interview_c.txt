79. Q:  What is the RAW system?
A:  The Router Approval Workflow (RAW) is an NDMS-based system that controls the digital review and approval of maintenance routers before any depot work begins. After a planner builds or modifies a route in RouteBuilder, it�s submitted through RAW for review by QA specialists, engineers, and production supervisors. Each reviewer verifies the sequence of operations, references, and procedures to ensure compliance with all maintenance and engineering standards.
The goal of RAW is to enforce configuration control and quality upfront. QA ensures inspection hold points are included, while engineering confirms that any deviations from manuals are properly authorized. Once all required approvals are complete, NDMS locks the router under configuration control � preventing unapproved edits and enabling authorized work orders. This digital approval chain replaces paper routing, speeds up workflow, and ensures every maintenance plan is properly vetted and documented before execution.

80. Q:  What is the EWO system?
A:  The Electronic Work Order (EWO) system is the execution side of NDMS. Once a maintenance router is approved, NDMS generates EWOs for each task in that route. Artisans use EWOs to access digital work instructions, technical references, and to electronically record their progress and signatures.
Quality Assurance (QA) inspectors also use the system at designated �hold points,� electronically signing off on required inspections before work can proceed. If discrepancies are found, the EWO routes the job back for correction, ensuring every action is documented.
By replacing paper work orders, the EWO system gives real-time visibility into production, improves traceability, and drastically reduces administrative delays. It also enhances accountability � every action, signature, and inspection is digitally recorded, providing a complete and auditable maintenance history for each aircraft or component.

81. Q:  What is the RouteBuilder system?
A: RouteBuilder is the planning backbone of NDMS, used to create and manage detailed maintenance routes for complex assets like aircraft or engines. Planners use it to break down each job into a sequence of operations � specifying work centers, technical references, required materials, estimated labor, and quality checkpoints.
RouteBuilder links every step to official manuals, drawings, and directives to ensure accurate execution on the shop floor. When updates are needed, planners can modify the route in RouteBuilder, which then enters the Router Approval Workflow (RAW) for review and authorization. In short, RouteBuilder turns maintenance requirements into clear, standardized work instructions that flow directly into the Electronic Work Order (EWO) system for execution.

82. Q:  How these components the Router Approval Workflow, RouteBuilder, and EWO system work together and their impact?
A:  These three systems work together to form the backbone of NDMS�s digital maintenance workflow. It begins with RouteBuilder, where planners create detailed maintenance routes that outline every operation, reference, and quality checkpoint needed for an aircraft or component. Once complete, the route moves into the Router Approval Workflow (RAW) for electronic review by QA, engineers, and supervisors. This ensures that every step meets technical, safety, and configuration standards before work begins. After approval, NDMS generates Electronic Work Orders (EWOs), which artisans and inspectors use to perform and document their tasks digitally.
This integration replaces paper-based processes with a seamless, traceable system. Approvals that once took days now happen in hours, and managers can monitor progress in real time. Each action, signature, and inspection is logged in NDMS, ensuring complete configuration control and accountability. The result is faster turnaround, better quality assurance, and stronger confidence that every aircraft or component leaving the depot meets Navy standards and is fully documented for future reference.

83. Q:  What is the Safe-For-Flight qualification?
A:  The Safe-for-Flight (SFF) qualification is one of the highest responsibilities in U.S. Naval Aviation maintenance. It authorizes designated personnel to certify that an aircraft is airworthy and ready for flight � effectively releasing it from maintenance to operational status. Only individuals formally designated by the Commanding Officer, after completing all required training and qualification standards, can sign the official aircraft acceptance record (OPNAV 4790/141), affirming that all maintenance, inspections, and checks meet Navy standards.
At Fleet Readiness Centers (like FRC Southeast), SFF-qualified personnel serve as the final authority before an aircraft goes to flight test. They review all documentation, verify configuration control, ensure QA inspections and ground checks are complete, and confirm that servicing and system parameters meet specifications. Once they sign off, the aircraft proceeds to a Functional Check Flight (FCF) with a test pilot.
Safe-for-Flight certification represents more than a technical qualification � it�s a position of deep trust and accountability. These individuals act as the final quality gate between maintenance and flight operations, ensuring every aircraft leaving the depot has been meticulously reviewed, tested, and verified safe for flight.

A: At the depot level, Discrepant Work Orders (DWOs) are the Navy�s formal method for capturing and correcting internal quality issues discovered during maintenance. Within NDMS, when a product, process, or assembly doesn�t meet technical standards, a Quality Control Inspector (QCI) initiates a DWO in the Quality Assurance Workbench (QAWB) to document and control the discrepancy.
From the QCI�s perspective, DWOs are more than administrative entries � they�re a critical quality gate. When a QCI finds a non-conformance such as a part out of tolerance or an improper process step, they open a DWO, classify the defect as critical, major, or minor, and coordinate corrective actions with production and QA. They verify rework, update NDMS documentation, and only close the DWO once the defect has been corrected and the product fully meets specifications.
There are two main categories of DWOs. In-Process DWOs are issued when a defect is caught within the same shop before the product leaves, while Reprocess DWOs occur when a defect is discovered after the product has moved on � indicating the issue escaped its original inspection. QA uses data from both to measure internal quality and target areas for improvement. Frequent reprocess DWOs may signal training or inspection gaps, while in-process DWOs show strong shop-level quality awareness.
Ultimately, DWOs provide traceability, accountability, and continuous feedback across the maintenance process. They ensure every discrepancy is documented, corrected, and learned from. QCIs � acting as the eyes and ears of QA � are central to that mission, safeguarding compliance, consistency, and the Navy�s high standards of airworthiness at the depot level.
